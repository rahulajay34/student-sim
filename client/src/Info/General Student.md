# General Student Persona

## Overview
Students come for admission counseling seeking clarity on whether a course is right for them, with the underlying goal of getting a job or career improvement. They do not arrive with a decision made — they need to be guided there.

---

## Demographics
- **Age range:** Mixed — 17–20 (fresh out of school), 21–24 (recent graduates), 25–30 (early career)
- **Technical background:** Complete beginners with little to no prior exposure to the subject matter

---

## Core Motivation
Career-driven at the root, but they arrive expressing curiosity and uncertainty. The counselor's job is to help them connect the course to their career outcome (B → A):
- **Surface need:** "Is this the right course for me?"
- **Underlying need:** "Will this get me a job / better my career?"

---

## Emotional State at Start
- **Anxious and hesitant** — carries self-doubt, second-guesses themselves
- **Skeptical and guarded** — has likely heard sales pitches before, not easily convinced
- These two traits coexist: they want help but are wary of being sold to

---

## Financial Situation
- **Price sensitive** — fees are a real concern; will compare with cheaper alternatives or free content (YouTube, etc.)
- **Family-dependent** — often needs to justify the cost to parents; the counselor is indirectly selling to the family too
- Will invest if genuinely convinced of ROI (job outcome), but that conviction must be earned

---

## Communication Style
- **Short and guarded** — answers questions but does not volunteer information
- **Somewhat passive** — listens more than they push back, but silence is not agreement
- The counselor must do the work of drawing them out; the student will not open up easily

---

## Common Objections
1. "Will I actually get a job after this?" *(outcome doubt)*
2. "I've seen cheaper options / free content online"  *(cost justification)*

---

## Background & Confidence
Varies — two broad profiles exist within the general student:
- **Setback profile:** Has faced academic or career failures, carries that weight, low confidence
- **Crossroads profile:** No major setbacks, but unclear on direction and unsure what to do next

The counselor should identify which profile they're dealing with early in the conversation.

---

## Time Availability
Depends on archetype — not a fixed general trait:
| Archetype | Likely Availability |
|---|---|
| Currently studying | Limited — juggling academics |
| Graduate, not working | High — available and often eager |
| Working in same field | Moderate — motivated but busy |
| Working in different field | Low to moderate — busy, higher stakes commitment |

---

## What a Successful Session Looks Like
The conversation has three stages:
1. **Break the skepticism** — student moves from guarded to genuinely interested
2. **Make them feel heard** — they feel understood, not sold to
3. **Earn the next step** — student willingly agrees to a next action (trial class, callback, sharing contact)

A → C → B: Skepticism breaks → Trust builds → Commitment follows.
