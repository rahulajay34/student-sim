# Counsellor Playbook — How to Convince the Student

A practical guide for the mock counselling call: how to take a hesitant prospective
student from "just exploring" to confidently blocking their seat — **honestly**.

It is grounded in two things at once:
1. **How real admissions counselling works** (rapport → discovery → tailored pitch → resolve concerns → ask).
2. **How this simulator actually decides the student says "yes"** (the disposition model) and how it grades you (the 8-parameter report).

Master the first and the second follows.

> **The one non-negotiable rule: never lie or over-promise.** The student is sometimes instructed to slip in a "trap" question (a hidden integrity probe). One false guarantee (a guaranteed job, a fake refund, a made-up "today-only" deadline) is logged, tanks your Product Knowledge score, and is exactly what real compliance reviews catch. Genuine selectivity ("seats in this cohort are limited") is fine; **inventing** urgency is not. Honesty is not a constraint on convincing — done right, it *is* what convinces.

---

## 1. What "convinced" actually means here

The student does **not** flip to "yes" at a magic satisfaction-score number. They agree to pay only when they reach the **`ready`** state, which needs **three things at once**:

1. **Positive momentum** — your recent turns have been landing (helpful, specific, on-point), not vague or pushy.
2. **Zero open objections** — every concern the student raised has been genuinely addressed (not deflected).
3. **At least one concern was raised and worked through** — a student won't commit if you never surfaced and handled a single real worry. Skipping straight to "so, shall we book it?" without ever drawing out a concern does not convince.

How *hard* this is varies per student: a more **skeptical** persona on a more **hesitant** scenario takes more, specific reassurance, and each session has a fixed "personality" — so the same script won't land identically every time. You can't brute-force it; you earn it.

**Translation into behaviour:** build rapport → uncover their real situation and concerns → present the programme mapped to *them* → resolve each concern with concrete facts → then make a clear, low-pressure ask. The payment ask in converting calls typically comes **late** (around three-quarters of the way in), **after** concerns are settled — not in the first two minutes.

## 2. The five phases (with what earns and loses points)

The call moves through five phases. Each maps to graded parameters. You start every dimension at "competent"; you move **up** with specific, student-tailored moves and **down** when you ignore what the student just said.

### Phase 1 — Opening & Rapport
**Goal:** warm, human start; confirm who you're speaking to; set a light agenda.

- **Do:** greet warmly, use their name, introduce yourself, confirm they can hear you, and frame the call ("I just want to understand where you're at and answer your questions").
- **Earns points:** a warm personalised open with a clear agenda.
- **Loses points:** abrupt "so are you going to enrol?" openings; getting the name wrong repeatedly and never correcting; jumping straight into the pitch.
- A single name slip or a slightly scripted line is minor — recover and move on.

### Phase 2 — Needs Discovery (the highest-leverage phase)
**Goal:** understand the person before you sell anything. This is where most calls are won or lost.

Ask, and *listen to the answers*:
- Background — what they studied / what they do now.
- Goal — why they took the qualifier, what they want next (role change, promotion, switch into analytics/AI).
- Constraints — time per week, budget/affordability, family situation, English comfort, laptop/connectivity.
- Motivation — the "why now."

- **Earns points:** uncovering background **and** goal **and** the real constraints, then visibly using them later.
- **Loses points:** one or two generic questions then pitching anyway; ignoring an answer they just gave you.
- **Why it matters mechanically:** discovery is what lets you (a) raise and resolve a real concern, and (b) make the pitch personal — both required to reach `ready`.

### Phase 3 — Programme Presentation
**Goal:** explain the programme **mapped to what they just told you** — not a brochure dump.

- Lead with the outcome they care about, then back it with structure: curriculum, format, duration, faculty, certificate, placement *assistance*.
- Tie every point to their goal: "You said you want to move into an analytics role — modules 5–7 are exactly the business-analytics and ML pieces hiring managers look for."
- **Earns points:** crisp, structured, clearly relevant-to-this-learner presentation.
- **Loses points:** generic feature lists; wrong or shaky facts (see the fact sheet in §5).

### Phase 4 — Objection Handling & Negotiation (where you convince)
**Goal:** surface concerns, then resolve each with concrete specifics + empathy. See the objection playbook in §3.

- **Earns points:** fully resolving each concern with real facts/figures/proof + a workable alternative, calmly, no pressure.
- **Loses points (badly):** dismissing or talking past a concern; vague reassurance with no specifics; repeating the same line when they raise it again.
- **Mechanically:** an objection stays "open" until you actually address it. **Open objections block the sale.** Address it, confirm it's settled, and move on — don't keep re-opening a concern they've let go of.

### Phase 5 — Closing & the Payment Ask
**Goal:** once concerns are settled and they're warming, make a clear, confident, low-pressure ask for the **₹4,000 seat-block**.

- **Do:** ask plainly ("Shall I go ahead and block your seat with the ₹4,000 so you're in this cohort?"), explain exactly what that ₹4,000 does, and secure a concrete next step.
- **Earns points:** a confident, well-timed, low-pressure ask that secures a decision or firm next step.
- **Loses points:** never asking ("let me know if you're interested"); asking too early, before any concern is resolved; high-pressure cornering.
- **Timing:** ask **after** discovery + at least one resolved concern. If they say "let me think," that's usually a *signal an objection is still open* — find it (§4).

## 3. Objection playbook (honest, specific responses)

For each concern: **acknowledge → answer with a concrete fact → tie it back to their goal → check it's settled.** Never wave it away.

> The "honest line" below is the real, defensible position. Stay inside it — overstepping it is misselling.

**Fee / "it's expensive"**
- Honest line: total fee is a real investment; the call only asks for the small refundable-into-fee seat-block now.
- Move: reframe cost against the outcome and break it down (per month via EMI). "It's ₹4,000 today to hold your seat; the full programme works out to about ₹10,827 a month over six months — for an IIM-Ranchi-credentialed analytics + AI programme."
- Don't: invent discounts or a fake price hike.

**EMI / affordability**
- Honest line: EMI is available through NBFC lending partners; it carries interest — it is **not** guaranteed zero-cost.
- Move: give the real monthly number and who finances it. "We have EMI through our NBFC partners — around ₹10,827 a month for six months."
- Don't: promise "no-cost / 0% EMI."

**Parents / family ("I need to discuss at home")**
- Honest line: legitimate; help them carry the case home.
- Move: equip them — "Totally fair. What's the main thing they'll ask? Let me give you the exact numbers and outcomes so you can walk them through it." Offer to keep the seat blocked while they discuss.
- Don't: pressure them to hide it from family or "decide right now or lose it" (unless the limited-seat point is genuinely true).

**Time commitment ("I work full-time")**
- Honest line: ~8–10 hours/week; live classes need attendance (~75–90%); recordings exist only with prior permission and **don't** substitute for attendance.
- Move: map it to their week from discovery. "It's about 8–10 hours a week, mostly evenings/weekends — does that fit the schedule you described?"
- Don't: say "just watch recordings whenever and you'll still get attendance."

**Competing priorities / "is now the right time"**
- Honest line: their call; connect to their stated "why now."
- Move: tie back to the goal and the cohort start. "You said you want to switch within the year — this cohort gets you certified in six months, which lines up with that."

**Trust / legitimacy ("is this genuine?")**
- Honest line: IIM Ranchi (NIRF-ranked) in partnership with Masai; real faculty; a certificate programme.
- Move: name the institute, ranking, faculty, and exactly what the credential is.
- Don't: call it an IIM **degree**, or promise **alumni status** — it's a certification programme, not a degree, and it doesn't confer IIM alumni access.

**Job / placement ("will I get a job / a 20 LPA package?")** — the most common trap.
- Honest line: **placement assistance / opportunities, not a guaranteed job, salary, specific company, or guaranteed interviews.** Support is conditional on eligibility (attendance/marks).
- Move: be honest and still compelling. "I can't promise a specific job or salary — no one credible can. What we do give is real placement support: resume help, interview prep, and opportunities, provided you meet the attendance and assessment criteria. The outcome depends on the work you put in, and the programme is built to get you ready for it."
- Don't: guarantee a job, a CTC, interviews, shortlisting, or a named company.

**Course fit / relevance ("is this right for someone like me?")**
- Honest line: depends on their background/goal — use discovery.
- Move: map specific modules to their goal; if it genuinely isn't a fit, say so (that honesty builds trust and often converts later).

**Language / English**
- Honest line: delivered in English (with some Hinglish); on-demand teaching in any native language isn't guaranteed.
- Move: reassure on support and pace; be honest about the medium.

**Tech / access (laptop, tools)**
- Honest line: a laptop + connection are the learner's responsibility; free/forever access to third-party tools isn't guaranteed (especially after the course).
- Move: clarify what's provided vs. what they need.

**Refund / "what if I don't like it"**
- Honest line: per T&Cs, the booking amount is **non-refundable** (it's adjusted into the fee, not returned); there is no money-back/cooling-off promise.
- Move: de-risk with what's real — talk through fit *before* they pay so they're confident, rather than promising a refund.
- Don't: promise a refund or "fully refundable booking."

## 4. Reading "let me think about it"

A stall almost always means **an objection is still open** — your job is to find it, not to push.

- Ask gently: "Of course — is it the fee, the time, or something about the outcomes that's still on your mind?"
- Surface the real concern → resolve it with §3 → *then* re-ask.
- If they've genuinely raised something twice, don't keep circling it; confirm what would settle it, address that, and move forward.

## 6. What you must NOT say (instant misselling)

Each of these is a logged integrity failure:

- ❌ "I guarantee you'll get a job / a ₹X LPA package / placed at [company]."
- ❌ "Interviews are guaranteed for everyone."
- ❌ "The ₹4,000 is fully refundable / you get it back."
- ❌ "Full refund if you don't like it after a few classes."
- ❌ "It's basically an IIM **degree**" / "you get IIM **alumni** status."
- ❌ "Just watch recordings later and you'll still get attendance."
- ❌ "The certificate is automatic once you pay."
- ❌ "0% / no-cost EMI, no extra charges."
- ❌ "This price is only valid today, it goes up tomorrow" (when untrue).
- ❌ "All classes are taught directly by the IIM professors."
- ❌ "Unlimited 1:1 mentorship, anytime, even weekends" / "unlimited assessment retakes" / "forever access to recordings."

When tempted, swap the guarantee for the honest version — it lands better and scores better.

## 7. The 60-second self-check (the 8 things you're graded on)

After every practice call, you're scored 0–5 on these. Aim ~3.5+ on each:

1. **Rapport & Opening** — warm, personalised, clear agenda.
2. **Needs Discovery** — background + goal + constraints, actually used.
3. **Programme Presentation** — structured, accurate, tied to the learner.
4. **Objection Handling** — every concern resolved with specifics + empathy.
5. **Product Knowledge** — confident, correct facts; **no misselling**.
6. **Closing & Payment Ask** — clear, well-timed, low-pressure ask.
7. **Communication & Empathy** — clear, warm, responsive; not a monologue.
8. **Personalised Experience** — every key point mapped to *this* learner.

---

## 8. Sample call arc (≈ how a converting call flows)

1. Warm hello, use their name, confirm audio, set agenda. *(rapport)*
2. "Before I tell you anything — tell me about yourself and what made you take the qualifier." *(discovery: background, goal)*
3. Probe constraints: time per week, budget, family, English, laptop. *(discovery)*
4. Present the programme mapped to their goal; give the real numbers. *(presentation)*
5. They raise a concern (fee / time / placement) → acknowledge → answer with a concrete fact → tie to their goal → confirm it's settled. *(objection handling)*
6. Surface any remaining worry the same way until none are open.
7. Now they're warming → "Shall I block your seat with the ₹4,000 so you're confirmed for this cohort? Here's exactly what that does…" *(close)*
8. Handle one last practical question honestly → confirm next step.

Build it, earn it, ask for it — and never promise what isn't true.
